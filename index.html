A meathic story in EU

Introduction

Climate change is one of today's biggest challenge. Agriculture is an important contributor to climate change, notably meat consumption. Meat can be very expensive in terms of resources to produce: it takes 15'415 liters of water for a single kilogram of beef, and reducing meat consumption would be a desirable goal.
In this project, we aim to see if we can determine some of the drivers behind people's meat consumption. We first take a look at the general meat consumption in Europe:
-What is the overall trend ? Is there notable differences between countries ?
We then try to determine whether certain factors can be associated with meat consumption.
-Is Household income related to meat consumption ?
And by delving deeper into a specific country, France:
-Does the price of meat have an impact on meat consumption ? Is there difference between types of meat ? Does unemployment (as a proxy for recessions) ?
-Can we see an association between global warming related events, such as conferences, and meat consumption ?
Finally, we try to use these values to predict meat consumption (MAYBE).

Part 2 : Economic Situation

Wealth and Meat Consumption in Europe

As meat is typically expensive, we could expect an association between the wealth of a country and its meat consumption. We use GDP per capita to measure country wealth, and our data is from the OECD (link?). Let us first plot the GDP per capita over the last ten years.
-CHANGE GRAPH FONTS

We can see that GDP per capita has been growing for all countries. Do we also see an increase in meat consumption ? Let's plot the meat consumption per gdp per capita, for all countries and all dates from 2004 to 2013
-INCREASE FONT SIZE IF POSSIBLE AND ADD AXIS LABEL FOR GRAPH

We see what seems like a fairly strong correlation between gdp and meat consumption, with a correlation of VALUE and R-squared value of 0.3. From the regression line, we could roughly expect an increase of VALUE of meat consumption per VALUE increase of GDP per capita. This picture is however not quite right; we now plot the result also by year, and additionally group countries by clustering using k-means, with 3 clusters.
-INCREASE FONT SIZE IF POSSIBLE AND ADD AXIS LABEL FOR GRAPH

We can first note that the clustering is meaningful: the first group corresponds roughly to poorer countries (mostly eastern),the second group to richer EU countries, and the third to luxembourg. These groupings are also stable through time, with essentially only Spain and Greece moving from group 2 to group 1, which makes sense as these two countries have been particularly hit by the 2008 economic crisis. The poorer consume on average less meat than the richer countries, 69.69 vs 86.31 kg per capita in 2004. We can also see that poorer countries show a stronger variation, with 50 for Bulgaria and 90 for Hungary, whereas richer countries are more similar. Within these groupes, however, consumption does not increase much with time: the average for poor countries is 70.834 in 2013 and 86.97 for richer countries in 2013. This is despite the average GDP per capita increasing by almost 75% for poor countries and 39% for richer countries. How can we explain this result, seemingly paradoxical compared to the correlation we computed ?
-Countries that have been rich for longer eat more meat; but countries do not change their meat consumption by much, even as their income can grow quite fast
-This seems to indicate that meat consumption has a fairly high cultural/habits component to it
-Does economic situation still play a role ?
-We know from the first graph that meat consumption went down around 2007; which corresponds to the economic crisis, so we suspect economic considerations
