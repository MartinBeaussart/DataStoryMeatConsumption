Introduction

Climate change is one of today's biggest challenges. Agriculture is an important contributor to climate change, notably meat production. Meat can be very expensive in terms of resources to produce: it takes 15'415 liters of water for a single kilogram of beef, and reducing meat consumption would be a desirable goal.
In this project, we aim to see if we can determine some of the drivers behind people's meat consumption. We first take a look at the general meat consumption in Europe:
-What is the overall trend ? Who are the biggest meat eaters ? Is there a difference between types of meat ?
We then try to determine whether certain factors can be associated with meat consumption.
-Is the economic situation of a country, such as wealth or recession, related to consumption ?
And by delving deeper into a specific country, France:
-Does the price of meat have an impact on meat consumption ? Is there difference between types of meat ? Does unemployment (as a proxy for recessions) ?
-Can we see an association with global warming related events, such as conferences ?
Finally, we try to use these values to predict meat consumption (MAYBE).

Wealth and Meat Consumption in Europe

As meat is typically expensive, we could expect an association between the wealth of a country and its meat consumption. We use GDP per capita to measure country wealth, and our data is from the OECD (https://data.oecd.org/gdp/gross-domestic-product-gdp.htm). Let us first plot the GDP per capita over the last ten years.

We can see that GDP per capita has been growing for all countries. Do we also see an increase in meat consumption ? Let's plot the meat consumption per gdp per capita, for all countries and all dates from 2004 to 2013.

We see what seems like a fairly strong correlation between gdp and meat consumption, with a correlation of VALUE and R-squared value of 0.3. From the regression line, we could roughly expect an increase of VALUE of meat consumption per VALUE increase of GDP per capita. This picture is however not quite right; we now plot the result also by year, and additionally group countries by clustering using k-means, with 3 clusters.

We can first note that the clustering is meaningful: the first group corresponds roughly to poorer countries (mostly eastern),the second group to richer EU countries, and the third to Luxembourg. These groupings are also stable through time, with essentially only Spain and Greece moving from group 2 to group 1, which makes sense as these two countries have been particularly hit by the 2008 economic crisis. The poorer group consumes on average less meat than the richer countries, 69.69 vs 86.31 kg per capita in 2004. We can also see that poorer countries show a stronger variation, with 50 for Bulgaria and 90 for Hungary, whereas richer countries are more similar. Within these groupes, however, consumption does not increase much with time: the average for poor countries is 70.834 in 2013 and 86.97 for richer countries in 2013. This is despite the average GDP per capita increasing by almost 75% for poor countries and 39% for richer countries. How can we explain this result, seemingly paradoxical compared to the correlation we computed ?
We note that countries that have been rich for longer eat more meat; but countries do not change their meat consumption by much, even as their income can grow quite fast. This can mean that wealth impacts meat consumption in the long term but not in the short term, which can depend more on non-economic factors such as cultural habits. Interest in Climate Change and Meat Consumption

We now look to see if we can find a relation between interest in climate change and global warming and meat consumption in France. We could expect a higher interest to mean less consumption. We estimate the interest in climate change by using google trends, with search keywords for "climate change" and "global warming". Let us plot these results along with annotations for important dates and average meat meat consumption in France.

We first see a clear relation between interest and climate change related events such as the COP. But we can also note that meat consumption and interest move close together, altough the overall consumption is fairly stable. This can be most likely explained in the following way: a majority on the population keeps fairly constant eating habits, but a smaller portion, of maybe more environmentally conscious people, alter their meat consumption when climate change seems a more pressing matter. Let us now see what the tendency is for different types of meat.

Interest in climate change and meat consumption for different types of meat

We can note some interesting trends for different types of meat. Beef shows the strongest relation with climate change interest, and the change in consumption is actually fairly large, at almost 15%. Poultry consumption, on the other hand, does not show any relation and always increases, with an increase over the period of around 20% Finally, pork shows a pattern similar to meat, altough its total consumption is quite stable with a change of around 6%. These results are in accordance with what we would expect: beef consumption is the most environmentally taxing and is the one whose consumption is most altered, and chicken the least problematic. A potentially confounding factor could be economic situation, as the reduction in meat could also be related to beef being the most expensive and its consumption reduced during a recession.
